Abstract:
Finding mates is evolutionarily fundamental and low mate access may increase the risk of
psychopathologies. Most studies assume that our social environment provides us with access to
mates, and that only personal characteristics or sexual competition limits mating success,
without considering limited mate access as a result of the social context. The aim of this study
was to create and psychometrically evaluate a measure for mate access. We conducted an online
survey in a sample of 528 Finnish adults recruited via social media platforms. We evaluated a
hypothetical two-factor structure divided into encounters with preferred mates (E) and
perceived mating possibilities (P) through confirmatory factor analysis. With some small
adjustments, and after removing one item with poor factor loading, the hypothetical model
showed acceptable fit. As expected, the between-factor correlation was positive. We also
investigated the relationship between the mate access factors E and P, and current social
situation and negative affect by specifying two separate pathway models. High social
interacting was positively associated with E, but also with P. P was negatively associated with
negative affect. Overall, the mate access scale is a reliable and valid measure that could be
incorporated in evolutionary and psychological research on human mating.

Keywords: Mate access, mating possibilities, social environment, negative affect, validation,
evolutionary psychology

Finding the Mating Gear – The Development and Validation of a Mate Access Scale
Researchers have been investigating human mate selection for decades (e.g., Buss &
Barnes, 1986; Feingold, 1992). Several studies have investigated mating strategies (e.g., Buss &
Schmitt, 1993) and mating preferences for both short-term mates (e.g., Li & Kenrick, 2006) and
long-term mates (e.g., Buss et al., 2001). Other major research areas have focused on mate value
(Edlund & Sagarin, 2014) and sociosexual desire (Simpson & Gangestad, 1991). Still, most of
these studies assume that individuals generally enjoy free access to a pool of potential mates to
choose from, and that only personal characteristics or competition from same-sex individuals
limits mating success (e.g., Buss, 1988; Buunk & Fisher, 2009). There are, however, considerable
individual differences between us and the social situations we spend time in. These differences
affect the access we have to mates (Stone et al., 2007), and research has shown that not having a
partner is related to decreased psychological well-being (e.g., Mookherjee, 1997; Yan et al.,
2011). We, therefore, argue that poor mate access is related to experiences of negative affect. The
present study investigated mate access, and developed a measure for mate access.
Social Environment and its Effects on Mate Access
With regards to mate access (i.e., the availability of potential mates in one’s social
environment), we argue that there are three important components that need to be addressed in
the understanding of the mate access concept; current social situation, preferred encounters, and
perceived possibilities. First, current social situation indicates the objective number of
interactions we have with other people in general. Second, encounters refer to the number of
preferred encounters, that is, with people we regard as potential mates based on our mating
preferences. Finally, perceived possibilities refer to perceived sexual and/or romantic mating
possibilities between individuals, and this aspect also includes the perceived interest others have
in oneself.
The current social situation of an individual will affect the frequency of preferred
encounters with other individuals. The number of people one meets in total in everyday life is
highly relevant for mate access, as this number is most likely closely related to our number of
preferred encounters. The more people we meet in daily life, the more likely we are to encounter
an individual that we view as a potential mate. The objective frequency of meeting individuals of
the desired age and gender, including both brief (e.g., someone passing by in the street) and

continued encounters (e.g., a colleague at work) is, however, not equivalent to the assessed
frequency of preferred encounters.
A preferred encounter also requires that we assess the other individual as sexually or
romantically interesting for it to constitute an encounter with a potential mate. For example, a
person who leads a highly social life will inevitably meet more people on a daily basis, and,
therefore, more likely encounter potential mates compared to someone with a less busy social
agenda. We, therefore, argue that if we meet more people in general, there will most likely be
more potential mates in this pool of people. Encounters with potential mates in the current social
situation is affected by, for example, the sex ratio in the surrounding (Buss, 1988), the proportion
of single individuals (Buunk & Fisher, 2009), and the relative attractiveness of other individuals
competing for the same mates (Stone et al., 2007). Individuals who more frequently encounter
potential mates will, thereby, all other things being constant, have higher mate access than
individuals who less frequently encounter individuals of the desired age and sex. As an example,
the ratio of young men and women in parts of Asia, such as China, India and South-Korea, have
changed remarkably over the recent decades and these countries experience a significant surplus
of men relative to women (Jha et al., 2006; Park & Cho, 1995; Tuljapurkar et al., 1995). Also
rural areas of Western countries, such as Finland and Norway, tend to have fewer young women
than young men due to sex-biased outmigration and socioeconomic change (Jentoft, 1993; Pelto,
1973). Men in areas with few women are more likely to remain single and be unable to find a
potential mate or have children (Hudson & Den Boer, 2004).
When encountering a potential mate, sexual and/or romantic possibilities to mate must be
perceived mutually for the encounter to lead to a consensual sexual or romantic relationship. A
perceived possibility with a potential mate could be receiving any kind of sexual or romantic
invitation or flirt, including observing, smiling, nodding, gazing, touching, and smaller
interpersonal distances (e.g., Cunningham & Barbee, 2008; Hall & Gunnery, 2013; McCormick
& Jones, 1989; Renninger et al., 2004). Since women are choosier (i.e., more selective) than men
regarding potential mates (Buss & Schmitt, 1993) in heterosexual mating, this has led to a
traditional gender-stereotyped social script (e.g., Laner & Ventrone, 2000; Rose & Frieze, 1989),
where men typically pursue women rather than vice versa. Therefore, in heterosexual mating,
women are usually approached more frequently than men, and women might, as the more

commonly approached sex, perceive their mating possibilities as higher (Finkel & Eastwick,
2009).
We argue that the number of encounters with potential mates is likely also related to the
number of perceived possibilities. The more potential mates one interacts with, the more often
one will experience mutual sexual and/or romantic interest with a potential mate. Our perceived
possibilities are, therefore, related to the number of people we meet in our current social situation
in general, but even more so to our number of preferred encounters with people we consider
potential mates. For example, in a limited social situation with relatively few people, an
individual will not interact with many people in general, and, therefore, not encounter many
potential mates, and, consequently, the perceived possibilities with potential mates will also be
low. However, an individual could also meet a lot of people and encounter a lot of potential
mates, but still perceive their possibilities to mate as low. In other words, in a social situation
with frequent encounters with potential mates, a person might still receive few invitations from
said mates.
The Psychological Consequences of not Finding Mates
Research has shown that not having a partner is related to decreased psychological wellbeing (e.g., Mookherjee, 1997; Yan et al., 2011). A study by González Gutiérrez et al. (2005)
found that respondents with stable partners reported more positive affect and general well-being
than those without a partner or without a stable partner. We, therefore, expect that poor mate
access is related to experiences of negative affect. Accessing mates, with the ultimate goal of
reproduction, is evolutionarily fundamental. It is of such central psychological importance, that
vast research on difficulties in finding a mate indicates an increased risk of loneliness (De JongGierveld & Aalberts, 1980), poor self-esteem (Frazier et al., 1996; Katz et al., 1998) and negative
emotions, such as aggression (Downey et al., 2000) and depression (Yan et al., 2011). Not having
a partner is also related to reduced life satisfaction (Bailey & Snyder, 2002), life quality, and
psychological well-being (Glenn & Weaver, 1981; Mookherjee, 1997). Depressed individuals
estimate their self-perceived mate value lower than non-depressed individuals, and both
decreased self-perceived and peer-perceived mate value is associated with increased symptoms of
depression (Fisher et al., 2014; Kirsner et al., 2003). Moreover, psychological distress, decreased
life satisfaction and negative emotions, such as anger, frustration, sadness, and shame, can be the
result of rejection by potential mates and not finding a mate at all (e.g., Blackhart et al., 2009;

Leary et al., 2012; Rhoades et al., 2011). In line with this, rejection by potential mates
signiﬁcantly reduces self-perceived mate value, while mate acceptance indistinctively increases it
(Zhang et al., 2015). Since encountering potential mates and being rejected by them is related to
negative affect (Blackhart et al., 2009; Leary et al., 2012; Rhoades et al., 2011), we expect that
even if we frequently encounter potential mates, but still perceive our mating possibilities as low,
the risk of negative psychological consequences might still be high. To summarize, individuals in
current social situations where they do not encounter nor perceive possibilities with potential
mates, are more likely to experience negative affect related to limited mate access.
Individual Differences
Our individual differences affect the access we have to mates (Stone et al., 2007). Due to
individual differences in, for example, mate value (i.e., the individual characteristics which
affects the capacity to find and keep mates; Waynforth, 2001) and sociosexual desire (i.e. a
person's desire for and interest toward uncommitted sexual relationships; Simpson & Gangestad,
1991), we expect that there is also individual variation in mate access. Both of these individual
differences would, therefore, affect perceived possibilities, but also encounters with potential
mates, and, hence, the psychological consequences of not finding a mate.
If an individual has high mate value this individual will, all other things being equal, be
perceived as more attractive compared to someone of lower mate value (e.g., Collins, 2000;
Griffiths & Kunz, 1973; Little et al., 2006; Singh, 2002) and, therefore, perceive more mating
possibilities (Landolt et al., 1995). Hence, this individual will have higher mate access compared
to an individual of lower mate value. On the other hand, individuals of high mate value also tend
to be choosier and regard potential mate's qualities as more important (Edlund & Sagarin, 2010;
Tadinac & Hromatko, 2007), which, in turn, could decrease the amount of individuals perceived
as potential mates. Nevertheless, Conroy-Beam (2018) found that individuals of higher mate
value had more choice with mates and were better able to choose mates that aligned with their
mate preferences, compared to individuals of lower mate value. In other words, mate value
affects the standards we set on potential mates, and high mate value increases the likelihood of
finding a partner that matches our preferences. According to Edlund and Sagarin (2010),
individuals also seem to prefer mates of similar mate value. In summary, this suggests that
variations in mate value will affect the relationship between an individual’s current social

situation, and how the current social situation affects, an individual’s encounters with potential
mates, and perceived mating possibilities.
Sociosexual desire will, on the other hand, moderate the effect between perceived mating
possibilities and negative psychological consequences. The higher sociosexual desire an
individual has, the stronger the individual will yearn for perceived mating possibilities, since
sociosexual desire describes an individual’s yearn for engaging in sexual activity without
emotional bonding or commitment (Simpson & Gangestad, 1992). Sociosexual desire has also
been linked to mate selection in several studies (e.g., Penke & Asendorpf, 2008; Simpson &
Gangestad, 1992) and single individuals express higher sociosexual desire than their romantically
involved counterparts (Rodrigues & Lopes, 2017). Ostovich and Sabini (2004) also found that
sex drive is highly and positively correlated with sociosexual desire, and that both sex drive and
sociosexual desire are positively correlated with lifetime number of sex partners. Moreover,
Vrangalova and Ong (2014) conducted a study where they found that individuals with higher
sociosexual desire typically reported higher well-being, both in form of lower distress and higher
thriving, after having casual sex compared to not having casual sex. They found no such
differences among individuals with low sociosexual desire. Considering this, individuals with
higher sociosexual desire not only yearn for a higher number of perceived possibilities with
potential mates, but they will also suffer more from negative psychological consequences and
experience more negative affect if mating possibilities are not perceived. In sum, individuals of
high sociosexual desire, will suffer more from not having mate access, compared to individuals
with low sociosexual desire.
The Current Study
The aim of this study was to create a measure for mate access, and to psychometrically
evaluate this measure. Although many aspects of human mating (e.g., mate selection, mate value
and mate preferences) have been explored, no specific measure has been developed to measure
mate access. We hypothesized that the current social situation shapes the two mate access
components; encounters and perceived possibilities. We also hypothesized mate access to be
associated with experiences of negative affect, such as sadness, frustration, anger, and shame. In
order to measure mate access, we designed six items to measure the frequency of encounters and
seven items to measure perceived possibilities. The hypothesized two-factor model was examined

12

through confirmatory factor analysis. Based on these assumptions we made a few more specific
predictions:
i) Men and women, whose current social situation includes frequent interactions with other
people in general, will i a) report more encounters, and i b) perceive more possibilities, with
potential mates, compared to men and women whose current social situation includes few
interactions with other people. i c) Frequent interactions with others in one’s current social
situation will be more strongly associated with frequent encounters with potential mates, than
frequent interactions with others in one’s current social situation will be associated with
perceived mating possibilities.
ii) Men and women who frequently ii a) encounter, and ii b) frequently perceive possibilities,
with potential mates, will also report less negative affect, compared to men and women who
perceive few mating possibilities. ii c) Frequently perceived mating possibilities will be more
strongly associated with less negative affect, than frequent encounters with potential mates
will be associated with negative affect.
We also explored how mate access is related to mate value and sociosexual desire. We argue that
men and women who regard themselves as coveted mates will frequently perceive mating
possibilities and, therefore, report less negative affect compared to men and women who do not
regard themselves as coveted mates. Men and women who frequently perceive mating
possibilities will report less negative affect, especially if they are uninterested in uncommitted
sexual relationships, compared to men and women who perceive few mating possibilities.
Method
Ethical Permission
The current study received permission by the Faculty of Arts, Psychology and Theology
at Åbo Akademi University, and by the Ethical Review Board of Åbo Akademi University prior
to the start of data collection.
Respondents
The final sample consisted of 528 respondents (268 women and 252 men). Eight
respondents did not identify as either female or male. The age of the respondents ranged from 18
to 77 with the mean age being 33.69 (SD = 9.38). We recruited respondents through
advertisement targeting Finnish adults on Facebook. Due to a low initial participation by men we
modified the advertisement and specifically targeted Finnish men in two follow-up

DEVELOPMENT AND VALIDATION OF A MATE ACCESS SCALE

13

advertisements. We excluded respondents who did not respond to all questions used in the current
study. The completion rate was 66.24 %.
Measures
The questionnaire consisted of three different parts: a demographic survey including items
also probing the current social situation of the respondent, items used to measure mate access and
negative affect, as well as scales to measure self-perceived mate value and sociosexual desire.
Respondent demographics. We inquired about respondents’ age, gender, sexual
orientation, relationship status, education level, monthly income level, and number of inhabitants
of their county of residence. Responses were given using fixed alternatives, and answers to all
questions were mandatory.
Mate access. To develop a scale to measure mate access, we first devised 13 questions
that in various ways could probe an individual’s perceived access to potential mates. We started
by inspecting scales connected to mate value, sociosexual orientation and attitudes, such as the
Mate Value Scale (Edlund & Sagarin, 2014), the Mate Value Inventory (Kirsner et al., 2003), the
Self Perceived Mating Success Scale (Landolt et al., 1995), the Components of Mate Value
Survey (Fisher et al., 2014), and the revised Sociosexual Orientation Inventory (Penke &
Asendorpf, 2008). We did this to avoid potential overlap between our items and previous items as
we wanted to probe the respondent’s everyday mate access (i.e., the size of respondents’ potential
mating pool) and how challenging they perceived it to find mates. We did not want to directly
measure respondents’ self-perceived mate value or how coveted they are on the mating “market”.
After creating a first version of the questionnaire, we conducted a pilot study with 21
psychology students (not included in our final sample) to receive extensive and freely formulated
feedback on the items. The feedback concerned response options, how easily understandable the
items were, and whether response options suited the questions. Based on the feedback, we edited
the items and once more asked seven psychology students (from the same group of students who
gave feedback in the first place) to provide feedback. Again, we edited the items and response
options according to their feedback. After this, the final number of items was 13.
In the questionnaire, we defined a “potential partner” as any individual one might be
sexually or romantically interested in, considering their gender, age, and personality. We
instructed respondents to answer as if they were single even if they currently were in a
relationship. As we were not interested in measuring online-only relationships, we asked

DEVELOPMENT AND VALIDATION OF A MATE ACCESS SCALE

14

respondents to not count people they only meet on the Internet. If respondents engaged in onlinerelationships and later met these people in real life, these interactions were, however, measured
through the included questions. We also separated between mate access to sexual and romantic
relationships, as preferences for sex- or short-term mates (e.g., Schmitt, 2005) and preferences for
romantic- or long-term mates (e.g., Buss et al., 2001) tend to differ. Several items were, thus,
repeated to separately inquire about respondents’ mate access to both sexual and romantic
relationships.
The items were partitioned into two different thematic blocks. In the first block of items,
we probed respondents about encounters with potential mates. This block included questions
about how many mates the respondents meet and talk to on an average day or during a normal
weekend (e.g., “How many potential partners do you talk to on an average day? Do not count
persons you only meet on the internet.”). In the second block of items, we probed respondents
about how often they receive sexual invitations from mates in their daily life, how many mates
they think they could have compared to other people, as well as questions about how the
respondents themselves evaluated their chances to find a mate within a week or during a month
(e.g., “If you were looking for a new partner to have sex with, in what time do you think it would
be realistic for you to find one?”). The scale consisted of items designed to measure two facets of
mate access: everyday encounters with other adults and perceived access to potential sexual and
romantic relationships (See Appendix for original items and response options in Finnish).
Current social situation. To measure the current social situation of respondents, we
inquired about how many adults the respondents meet during a common workday, in their spare
time during a full week, and seeing friends and acquaintanceships during a full week (See
Appendix for original items and response options in Finnish).
Negative affect. To measure the respondents’ negative affect in response to not being able
to find a mate we probed respondents about negative emotions with respect to their perceived
mate access. We asked the respondents about how much sadness, frustration, anger, and shame
they felt as a result of their mate access regarding both sexual and romantic relationships (e.g.,
“How often are you frustrated over not finding a partner to have sex/a romantic relationship
with?”) (See Appendix for original items and response options in Finnish).

Self-perceived mate value. We used the Mate Value Scale (MVS; Edlund & Sagarin,
2014) to measure the respondents’ self-perceived desirability as a mate. The scale consists of one
item to measure one’s desirability as a mate, one item to measure desirability as a mate among
the other sex, one item to measure relative desirability as a mate (compared to other mates), and
one item to measure how good of a “catch” the respondent perceives oneself. Respondents
respond using a Likert-type scale ranging from 1 (“Extremely undesirable”/”Very much lower
than average”/”Very bad catch”) to 7 (“Extremely desirable/”Very much higher than
average”/”Very good catch”). According to Edlund and Sagarin (2014), the MVS is an efficient,
reliable, and valid self-report measure on mate value. They demonstrated MVS has excellent
internal consistency and test–retest reliability. The scale also has convergent validity with
previously demonstrated components of mate value and discriminant validity with a measure of
social desirability. The scale was translated from English to Finnish by two of the authors and the
translations merged. In our sample, the mean was 4.82 (SD = 1.57) and the internal reliability
adequate, .93 [.93, .94].
Sociosexual desire. We included the revised Sociosexual Orientation Inventory (SOI-R;
Penke & Asendorpf, 2008) to measure respondents’ sociosexual desire. The SOI-R is a revised
version the Sociosexual Orientation Inventory (Simpson & Gangestad, 1991). The SOI-R is a
measure of nine gender-neutral self-report items, of which three assess socio sexual desire by
probing i) fantasizing about sex with someone else than a partner, ii) experiencing sexual arousal
towards someone else than a partner, and iii) sexual fantasies about someone recently met. For
these three items, respondents respond using Likert-type scales ranging from 1 (“Never”) to 8
(“At least once a day”). According to Penke and Asendorpf (2008), the SOI-R has demonstrated
good internal consistency (Cronbach’s α =.83), high test-retest stability (r= .83 for men and r= .78
for women), and a confirmatory factor analysis of the SOI–R has shown that adequate factor
loadings. In our sample, the mean level of sociosexual desire was 4.18 (SD = 2.02) ranging from
1 to 9. The internal reliability was adequate, α = .87 [.85, .89].
Procedure
We created the online survey using the Survey Analytics platform and collected the data
during five days in March-April 2019 with the aim to gather at least 250 male and 250 female
respondents. We distributed a recruitment advert on Facebook and invited respondents to partake
in a survey concerning sexual and romantic partnerships. The respondents read and agreed to a

DEVELOPMENT AND VALIDATION OF A MATE ACCESS SCALE

18

standard informed consent form before starting. We assured respondents anonymity and informed
them that participation was voluntary and could be discontinued at any time. We also informed
respondents about the possibility of participating in a lottery with 4 gift cards a 50€ to a Finnish
chain store.
After asking respondents to provide demographic information, we asked them to respond
to our items probing their current social situation and mate access. Next, we asked the
respondents to complete questions about negative affect regarding not being able to find a mate,
the SOI-R, and the MVS 1. The final page included a link to another site, were respondents could
choose to participate in the lottery of the four gift cards by submitting their e-mail address.

Discussion
The objective of this study was to develop a measure for mate access and
psychometrically evaluate the scale. Research has been investigating human mate selection for
decades (e.g., Buss & Barnes, 1986; Feingold, 1992). But little research has, however, focused on
mate access, although accessing mates is evolutionarily fundamental and difficulties in finding a
mate have been linked to negative psychological consequences (e.g., Bailey & Snyder, 2002;
González Gutiérrez et al., 2005). We therefore hypothesized that mate access is highly relevant
for psychological well-being, and that individuals who do not encounter nor perceive possibilities
with potential mates, are more likely to experience negative affect due to limited mate access. For
the purpose of this study, we developed a mate access scale. We inspected existing scales related
to mate access and created an online survey targeting Finnish adults to probe the respondents
with questions about encounters with potential mates and perceived mating possibilities. The
final scale consisted of 12 items, divided into two conceptual blocks, and the final sample of 528
respondents with an equal distribution between women and men (268 women and 252 men).
Main Findings and Interpretations
We evaluated the hypothetical two-factor structure divided into Encounters (E) and
perceived Possibilities (P) through confirmatory factor analysis. As was expected, the betweenfactor correlation was high and positive, and the results showed that difficulties in finding a
sexual/romantic partner is associated with negative affect. The findings indicated that individuals
who did not perceive mate access to potential mates that they, based on their gender, age and
personality, could be sexually/romantically interested in, reported more negative affect compared

DEVELOPMENT AND VALIDATION OF A MATE ACCESS SCALE

27

to individuals who did perceive mate access to potential mates. Moreover, as hypothesized,
interacting with a lot of people in general was positively associated with encounters and with
perceived possibilities with potential mates. Encounters were also positively associated with
experiences of negative affect, while perceived possibilities were negatively associated with
negative affect. Higher values for current social situation, encounters and perceived possibilities
indicated better access to mates, and for negative affect, higher values indicated more negative
affect. In other words, the more encounters with potential mates a respondent reported, the higher
were their perceived possibilities to access mates, and the less perceived mating possibilities a
respondent reported, the more negative affect they experienced. The results also showed that a
more usable scale of the mate access measure with mean composite scores can be used as an
observed (as opposed to latent) measure without much information loss. Overall, the mate access
scale is a reliable and valid measure that could be incorporated in evolutionary and psychological
research.
Supporting our hypotheses, we found that current social situation was positively associated
with perceived possibilities, and even more so with encounters. The associations were expected,
since individuals who meet a lot of people in general in their daily life are more likely to
encounter more potential mates and to perceive mating possibilities with some of the potential
mates they encounter. In other words, respondents who met more people in their daily life
perceived their mate access as higher. Even very brief and/or professional encounters will more
likely lead to this perception, since the individual will be more aware of the existence of possible
mates. For example, someone working in a coffee shop will probably encounter more people than
someone working in a factory, and, therefore, is more likely to experience mate abundance and a
larger mate access.
Similarly, in line with our hypotheses, perceived possibilities were strongly negatively
associated with negative affect. But when controlling for the association between the perceived
possibilities and negative affect, the encounters were weakly positively associated with negative
affect. This could be due to respondents encountering a lot of people, but not perceiving mating
possibilities with or being rejected by these encountered potential mates, and, therefore, still
experiencing negative affect (e.g., Blackhart et al., 2009; Leary et al., 2012; Rhoades et al., 2011).
Based on this study, low mate access elicited negative affect in the respondents. The more the
respondents perceived mating possibilities as poor, the more negative were their affects.

Individual Differences
Next, we tested whether self-reported mate value moderated the association between
current social situation and perceived possibilities. We found that mate value was a significant
predictor of perceived mating possibilities with potential partners. This suggests that individuals
who perceived themselves to have a higher mate value, that is, be more appealing to others, also
perceived themselves to be more likely to convert their encounters with potential mates into
actual mating opportunities. This interaction was, however, not statistically significant. The
interaction would be expected, since individuals of higher mate value are more coveted on the
mating market (Edlund & Sagarin, 2014) and better at finding mates that align with their mate
preferences (Conroy-Beam, 2018). Nevertheless, research has also shown than individuals tend to
approach (Penke & Denissen, 2008) and be in happier relationships (Conroy-Beam et al., 2016)
with people of similar mate value compared to themselves as a mechanism of avoiding rejection.
This could explain why this interaction was not statistically significant, and respondents of higher
mate value did not significantly experience more mate access.
After this, we also investigated the expected interaction between sociosexual desire and
perceived possibilities in predicting negative affect. The results showed that both lower perceived
possibilities with potential partners and higher sociosexual desire predicted stronger negative
affect. These interactions were also expected, since sociosexual desire is a motivational state that
is characterized by heightened sexual interest and targeted specifically at potential mates to
whom the individual has no committed romantic relationship (Simpson, Wilson & Winterheld,
2004; Simpson & Gangestad, 1991). Regarding this, it would be expected that respondents who
reported high sociosexual desire also would be more likely to suffer from low mate access.
The results also indicated some gender differences. We found that women reported less
encounters with potential mates than men, but when controlling for difference in encounters,
women perceived more possibilities to convert encounters into actual mating opportunities than
men. This could be explained both by women being choosier than men regarding potential
partners (Buss & Schmitt, 1993), and by men more often approaching women than vice versa
(e.g., Laner & Ventrone, 2000; Rose & Frieze, 1989). Because of this, women, as the more
approached sex, perceive their mating possibilities as higher than men. Men, on the other hand, as
the more approaching sex, perceive more encounters with potential mates due to not being as

29

selective as women, but also because they do not receive as many sexual/romantic invitations
from potential partners as women do.
Limitations of the Current Study
There are some limitations or considerations in the study that need to be noted. The first
issue concerns the measure of the encounters. This measure was limited to face to faceencounters. Even greater mate access is available in online dating services, such as Tinder and
match.com, which enable single people to assess tens of thousands of potential mates (e.g.
Lenton et al., 2008). Recently, online dating has increased significantly. The dynamics of online
dating are, however, very different from the dynamics of traditional dating and both mate access
and mate value are modified by online dating. Online dating was, therefore, not addressed in this
study. In this study only one’s real life mate access and the people one meets face to face were
addressed. In an attempt to only measure real life encounters, we requested respondents not to
count persons they meet on the internet. In spite of this, we can’t be completely sure that this
request was considered by respondents and it might have been quite challenging for respondents
to distinguish between persons they only talk to online and in real life.
Secondly, the definition of a “potential partner” might have been ambiguous to
respondents. In the questionnaire we defined potential partners as “individuals you might be
sexually/romantically interested in, considering their gender, age, and personality”. It might have
been difficult for respondents to keep the definition in mind throughout answering the
questionnaire. To limit this impact, we reminded participants of the definition by repeating the
definition at four different times throughout the questionnaire, but respondents might still have
interpreted “potential partner” in slightly different ways or inconsistently throughout the
questionnaire.
Finally, we asked respondents to do their best at answering the questions as if they were
single, even if they currently were in a romantic relationship. This might, however, have been
difficult for people in long-term romantic relationships to imagine. When comparing single
respondents with respondents currently in a relationship, we found that single respondents
reported both less encounters with potential mates and less perceived mating possibilities after
controlling for encounters. Another possible explanation for this finding might also be that
respondents in relationships had higher mate value than single respondents. Mates of high mate
value are more likely to use strategies that benefit their partners, which make them even more

DEVELOPMENT AND VALIDATION OF A MATE ACCESS SCALE

30

valuable as mates and, consequently, increases relationship satisfaction, makes them good mates
(Salkicevic et al., 2014), and decreases the risk of becoming single. Moreover, the unrestricted
sociosexual desire that is measured by the SOI-R is specifically targeted at potential mates to
whom no committed romantic relationship exists (Simpson & Gangestad, 1991; Simpson et al.,
2004). All the desire subscale items in SOI-R are about uncommitted sexual partners and these
questions might have been answered in different ways by single respondents and respondents in
relationships.
Conclusions
The findings from this item scale study with a Finnish adult sample showed that the mate
access scale is a reliable and valid two-factor measure of mate access. We found that the mean
composite scores can be used as an observed measure without much information loss. The results
indicated that difficulties in finding a mate and experiences of low mate access might elicit
negative affect in individuals. To the authors’ knowledge no previous studies have explored mate
access and its possible psychological consequences, nor developed or psychometrically evaluated
a measure for the assessment of mate access.
Future studies should, however, validate the scale in other settings and samples. For
instance, self-perceived mate access, negative affect, and mate value might co-vary in other ways
in much larger cities or much smaller towns than the ones explored in this study. If replicated,
other samples in bigger cities or countries could be used. The mate access scale could be used in
addition to, for example, scales created to measure self-perceived mate value as an explaining
complement and related phenomenon. We believe that this measure could be incorporated in
evolutionary and psychological research to further understand how this construct influences
human thinking, affect, and behavior.

Då man möter en potentiell partner måste även sexuella eller romantiska möjligheter
uppfattas ömsesidigt för att mötet ska kunna leda till en sexuell eller romantisk interaktion. En
upplevd parningsmöjlighet med en potentiell partner kan vara vilken som helst slags sexuell eller
romantisk inbjudan eller flört, inklusive leenden, beröring, minskad mellankroppslig distans och
förlängd ögonkontakt (t.ex. Cunningham & Barbee, 2008; Hall & Gunnery, 2013; McCormick &
Jones, 1989; Renninger et al., 2004). Antagandet om att kvinnor är mer kräsna (d.v.s. mer
selektiva) än män beträffande potentiella partners (Buss & Schmitt, 1993) har lett till ett
könsstereotypt socialt manus (t.ex. Laner & Ventrone, 2000; Rose & Frieze, 1989), där män
vanligtvis är de som gör närmanden mot kvinnor. På grund av att kvinnor oftare närmas av män
än tvärtom, kan kvinnors parningsmöjligheter uppfattas som fler (Finkel & Eastwick, 2009) än
mäns parningsmöjligheter.
Eftersom det är evolutionärt grundläggande för vår reproduktion att hitta en partner, kan
svårigheter med att hitta en partner således öka risken för psykopatologier. Forskning indikerar
att svårigheter med att hitta en partner ökar risken för ensamhet (De Jong-Gierveld & Aalberts,
1980), lågt självförtroende (Frazier et al., 1996; Katz et al., 1998), aggression (Downey et al.,
2000) och depression (Yan et al., 2011). Att inte ha en partner är även förknippat med minskad
livstillfredsställelse (Bailey & Snyder, 2002), livskvalitet och psykologiskt välbefinnande (Glenn
& Weaver, 1981; Mookherjee, 1997). Deprimerade personer uppskattar också sitt
självrapporterade partnervärde lägre än icke-deprimerade personer och ett lågt partnervärde
överlag är förknippat med depressiva symtom (Fisher et al., 2014; Kirsner et al., 2003). Studier
har också visat på att upplevelser av att bli avvisad av en potentiell partner eller att inte hitta en
partner överhuvudtaget kan resultera i psykologisk stress, minskad livstillfredsställelse och
negativa emotioner (Blackhart et al., 2009; Leary et al., 2012; Rhoades et al., 2011). Dessutom
leder avvisning av potentiella partners till en signifikant minskning i det självrapporterade
partnervärdet, medan acceptans av potentiella partners har motsatt effekt (Zhang et al., 2015).
Sammanfattningsvis är individer i sociala kontexter där de varken möter potentiella partners eller
upplever parningsmöjligheter mer sannolika att lida av negativ affekt p.g.a. låg partnertillgång.
På grund av individuella skillnader mellan personer, så som partnervärde (d.v.s.
individuella karakteristika som påverkar ens förmåga att hitta och behålla partners (Waynforth,
2001)) och sociosexuell lust (d.v.s. en persons intresse för och önskan att ha tillfälliga sexuella
relationer) (Simpson & Gangestad, 1991)), finns det också stor variation i hur ofta vi möter

potentiella partners och upplever parningsmöjligheter. Eftersom individer med högt partnervärde
uppfattas som mer attraktiva (t.ex., Collins, 2000; Griffiths & Kunz, 1973; Little et al., 2006;
Singh, 2002) och uppfattar fler parningsmöjligheter (Landolt et al., 1995) än individer med lågt
partnervärde, kommer individer med högt partnervärde även att ha högre partnertillgång än
individer med lågt partnervärde (om övriga förhållanden hålls konstanta). Partnervärde kommer
således att påverka relationen mellan en individs tillfälliga sociala kontext, och hur den tillfälliga
sociala kontexten påverkar individens möten, och parningsmöjligheter med potentiella partners.
Sociosexuell lust, å andra sidan, modererar effekten mellan upplevda parningsmöjligheter
och negativa psykologiska följder. Ju högre sociosexuell lust en individ har, desto starkare önskan
har individen också för upplevda parningsmöjligheter (Simpson & Gangestad, 1992). Enligt
Vrangalova och Ong (2014) rapporterar individer med hög sociosexuell lust mer välmående efter
att de haft en tillfällig sexuell relation, än då de inte haft det. Individer med högre sociosexuell
lust kommer således antagligen även lida mer av negativ affekt om de inte upplever
parningsmöjligheter och, slutligen, lida mer av låg partnertillgång än individer med låg
sociosexuell lust.
Studiens Syfte
Syftet med denna studie var att skapa ett mått på partnertillgång och att psykometriskt
utvärdera detta mått. Trots att många aspekter av partnerval har studerats har inget specifikt mått
för partnertillgång utvecklats. För att mäta partnertillgång konstruerade vi sex delfrågor för att
mäta antalet möten med potentiella partners och sju delfrågor för att mäta subjektivt upplevda
parningsmöjligheter. Vi förväntade oss även att partnertillgång påverkas av den tillfälliga sociala
kontexten och att låg partnertillgång är förknippat med negativ affekt. På basen av dessa
antaganden gjorde vi ett antal mer specifika prediktioner:
i)

Män och kvinnor vars tillfälliga sociala kontext inkluderar frekventa interaktioner
med andra personer kommer att i a) rapportera fler möten och i b) rapportera fler
parningsmöjligheter, jämfört med män och kvinnor vars tillfälliga sociala kontext
inkluderar få interaktioner med andra personer. i c) Frekventa interaktioner med andra
personer i ens tillfälliga sociala kontext kommer att vara starkare förknippade med
frekventa möten med potentiella partners, än vad frekventa interaktioner med andra i
ens tillfälliga sociala kontext kommer att vara förknippade med upplevda
parningsmöjligheter.

ii)

Män och kvinnor som frekvent ii a) möter potentiella partners och ii b) upplever
parningsmöjligheter, kommer också att rapportera mindre negativ affekt jämfört med
män och kvinnor som sällan upplever parningsmöjligheter. ii c) Frekvent upplevda
parningsmöjligheter kommer att vara starkare förknippade med mindre negativ affekt,
än frekventa möten med potentiella partners.

I studien undersökte vi även hur partnervärde och sociosexuell lust är associerade med
partnertillgång.
Metod
Denna studie erhöll tillstånd av Fakulteten för humaniora, psykologi och logopedi vid
Åbo Akademi och av den etiska granskningsnämnden vid Åbo Akademi före datainsamlingen
påbörjades. Det slutgiltiga samplet bestod av 528 respondenter, vilka rekryterades via sociala
medier. För att utveckla ett mått på partnertillgång inspekterade vi skalor för närliggande koncept,
så som, eftersom vi ville undvika överlapp med andra skalor. Vi ämnade undersöka
respondenternas partnertillgång i deras vardagliga liv (d.v.s. storleken på deras pool av potentiella
partners) och hur utmanande de anser det vara att hitta partners. I frågeformuläret definierade
vi ”potentiell partner” som vilken individ som helst som man kan tänka sig själv vara sexuellt
eller romantiskt intresserad av, på basen av deras kön, ålder och personlighet. Vi bad
respondenterna svara som om de vore singlar, även om de för tillfället var i en relation. Eftersom
vi inte var intresserade av internetbaserade relationer, bad vi även respondenterna att inte
rapportera personer de endast träffar online. Delfrågorna delades upp i två tematiska block. I det
första blocket utredde vi respondenternas möten med potentiella partners och i det andra blocket
hur ofta de får sexuella/romantiska förslag från potentiella partners. För att mäta deltagarnas
tillfälliga sociala kontext frågade vi respondenterna hur många vuxna de träffar på en vanlig
arbetsdag, på deras fritid under en vanlig vecka och hur många vänner och bekanta de träffar
under en vecka. För att mäta negativ affekt som uppstår hos deltagarna p.g.a. låg partnertillgång
frågade vi respondenterna om hur ofta de upplever ledsenhet, frustration, ilska eller skam p.g.a.
svårigheter med att hitta en sex- eller romantisk partner (Se Appendix för delfrågor och
svarsalternativ). För att mäta respondenternas sociosexuella lust använde vi oss av den reviderade
versionen av ”Sociosexual Orientation Inventory” (SOI-R) av Penke och Asendorpf (2008) och
för att mäta respondenternas självrapporterade partnervärde använde vi oss av Mate Value Scale

(MVS) av Edlund och Sagarin (2014). Vi skapade ett frågeformulär på den internetbaserade
plattformen ”Survey Analytics” och samlade in data under fem dagar i mars-april 2019.
De statistiska analyserna genomfördes med programmet R (R Development Core Team,
2008). För att visualisera korrelationer mellan observerade responser på delfrågorna använde vi
paketet corrplot (Wei, 2016). Vidare utförde vi en konfirmatorisk faktoranalys med lavaanpaketet (Rosseel, 2012). Eftersom data ansågs vara på ordinalskalenivå använde vi oss av robust
WLS (WLSMV) estimering med deltaparametrisering. För att kartlägga strukturella regressioner
använde vi paketet semPlot (Epskamp, 2014) och för att testa associationer mellan sammansatta
skalor använde vi lme4-paketet (Bates et al., 2015). Data, R-skript och outputs är publicerade på
Open Science Framework (Hemsida).
Resultat
Vi utvärderade en hypotetisk två-faktorstruktur, uppdelad i möten (E) med potentiella
partners och upplevda parningsmöjligheter (P), med konfirmatorisk faktoranalys. Vi undersökte
två modeller. Med små justeringar och efter att ha avlägsnat en delfråga med svag faktorladdning
visade den hypotetiska modellen nästintill acceptabel passform. Som förväntat var
mellanfaktorkorrelationerna höga och positiva. Vi undersökte även relationen mellan
partnertillgångsfaktorerna E och P samt den nuvarande sociala kontexten och negativ affekt
genom att specificera två separata modeller. Att interagera med människor överlag visade sig vara
positivt associerat med möten, men också med upplevda parningsmöjligheter. Upplevda
parningsmöjligheter var negativt associerade med negativ affekt, medan möten med potentiella
partners var positivt associerade med negativ affekt. Resultaten visade också att summapoäng kan
användas som ett observerat mått utan att förlora information.
Vidare var partnervärde en signifikant prediktor för upplevda parningsmöjligheter, vilket
tyder på att individer som ansåg sig själva vara mer eftertraktade som partners i jämförelse med
andra, sannolikt också är bättre på att konvertera sina möten med potentiella partners till verkliga
parningsmöjligheter. Interaktionen mellan partnervärde och parningsmöjligheter var däremot inte
statistiskt signifikant. Även både få upplevda parningsmöjligheter och stark sociosexuell lust var
förknippade med starkare negativ affekt. Interaktionen mellan upplevda parningsmöjligheter och
sociosexuell lust var statistiskt signifikant. Resultaten indikerade att respondenter som rapporterat
både låg partnertillgång och stark sociosexuell lust led mest av negativ affekt. Kvinnor
rapporterade färre möten med potentiella partners än män, men då vi kontrollerade för antalet

36

möten upptäckte vi att kvinnor, de facto, upplevde fler parningsmöjligheter. Singlar rapporterade
såväl färre möten med potentiella partners, som parningsmöjligheter, jämfört med respondenter i
förhållanden. Det fanns ingen statistisk skillnad i vare sig möten med potentiella partners eller
upplevda parningsmöjligheter mellan hetero- och icke-heterosexuella respondenter.
Diskussion
Syftet med denna studie var att utveckla en ny skala för att mäta partnertillgång och att
psykometriskt utvärdera detta mått. Resultaten antydde, i enlighet med våra förväntningar, att
mellanfaktorkorrelationerna var höga och positiva. I enlighet med våra hypoteser var frekventa
interaktioner med personer överlag korrelerat med möten med potentiella partners och med
upplevda parningsmöjligheter. Höga värden på tillfällig social kontext, möten med potentiella
partners och upplevda parningsmöjligheter indikerade bättre partnertillgång, medan höga värden
på negativ affekt indikerade mer negativ affekt.
I enlighet med våra hypoteser var tillfällig social kontext positivt associerat med möten,
men även med upplevda parningsmöjligheter. Associationen var förväntad, eftersom individer
som interagerar med fler personer i sin vardag sannolikt också möter fler potentiella partners och
därmed upplever fler situationer där de kan konvertera dessa möten till verkliga
parningsmöjligheter. I likhet med detta och i enlighet med våra hypoteser var upplevda
parningsmöjligheter starkt negativt associerade med negativ affekt och associationen var starkare
än associationen mellan möten och negativ affekt. I motsats till vår fjärde hypotes var möten
svagt positivt associerad med negativ affekt, vilket kan bero på att respondenter som möter
många personer men trots detta inte upplever parningsmöjligheter eller blir avvisade, upplever
mer negativ affekt än andra (t.ex., Blackhart et al., 2009; Leary et al., 2012; Rhoades et al., 2011).
Sammanfattningsvis, ju lägre partnertillgång respondenterna rapporterade, desto mer negativ
affekt rapporterade de.
Resultaten indikerade även att individer med högt självskattat partnervärde var bättre på
att omvandla möten med potentiella partners till verkliga parningsmöjligheter. Resultaten var
förväntade, eftersom individer med högre partnervärde ses som mer eftertraktade partners
(Edlund & Sagarin, 2014) och därmed antagligen får fler sexuella/romantiska förslag.
Interaktionen var inte signifikant, vilket skulle kunna förklaras av att vi tenderar närma oss
(Penke & Denissen, 2008) och inleda relationer (Conroy-Beam et al., 2016) med personer av
liknande partnervärde som vi själva. Resultaten antydde även att både få upplevda

37

parningsmöjligheter och hög sociosexuell lust var förknippade med särskilt stark negativ affekt.
Interaktionen var statistiskt signifikant och kan förklaras av att personer med hög sociosexuell
lust önskar ha fler interaktioner med potentiella partners än personer med låg sociosexuell lust.
Vidare tydde resultaten på att kvinnor upplevde fler parningsmöjligheter, men endast då vi
kontrollerade för möten med potentiella partners. Fyndet kunde förklaras av att kvinnor ofta är
mer selektiva än män angående potentiella partners (Buss & Schmitt, 1993) och av att män oftare
gör sexuella/romantiska närmanden mot kvinnor än tvärtom (Laner & Ventrone, 2000; Rose &
Frieze, 1989).
Till begränsningarna i denna studie hör att den partnertillgång vi ämnar mäta är begränsad
till potentiella partners man träffar ansikte-mot-ansikte, vilket betyder att alla interaktioner med
potentiella partners på internet exkluderas. Internetbaserade dejtingsajter erbjuder dess användare
möjlighet att interagera med tiotusentals potentiella partners (Lenton et al., 2008). Dynamiken i
online-baserad dejting är däremot mycket olik dynamiken i traditionell dejting, vilket gör dessa
vettiga att studera separat. Även om vi uppmanat respondenterna att endast rapportera möten och
parningsmöjligheter som ägt rum utanför internet kan dessa ha varit svåra att separera för
respondenterna.
Vidare kan definitionen av en ”potentiell partner” ha uppfattats som tvetydig av
respondenter. I frågeformuläret definierade vi potentiella partners som ”individer du skulle kunna
vara sexuellt/romantiskt intresserad av, med avseende på deras kön, ålder och personlighet”.
Definitionen kan ha uppfattats som utmanande för respondenter att hålla i minnet. Dessutom kan
respondenter ha tolkat potentiell partner inkonsekvent, trots att vi upprepade definitionen fyra
gånger på olika ställen i frågeformuläret för att motverka dessa effekter. Slutligen bad vi
respondenterna att göra sitt bästa för att svara på frågorna som om de vore singlar. Då vi jämförde
respondenter som rapporterat att de var singlar med respondenter som rapporterat att de var i ett
förhållande visade resultaten att singlar rapporterade färre möten med potentiella partners än
respondenter i förhållanden. Detta skulle kunna tyda på att det var utmanande för respondenter
som för tillfället var i förhållanden att föreställa sig vara singel. En annan möjlig förklaring skulle
kunna vara att respondenter i förhållanden hade högre partnervärde än singla respondenter,
eftersom individer med högre partnervärde tenderar att vara bättre partners än individer med lågt
partnervärde (Salkicevic et al., 2014) och således minskas risken för att de blir singlar igen.
Dessutom är underskalan som ämnar sociosexuell lust i SOI-R fokuserad på tillfälliga sexuella

DEVELOPMENT AND VALIDATION OF A MATE ACCESS SCALE

38

relationer (Simpson & Gangestad, 1991; Simpson et al., 2004) och frågorna kan därför ha
besvarats på olika sätt av respondenter som uppgett olika förhållandestatus.
Sammanfattningsvis visade resultaten att svårigheter med att hitta en partner, d.v.s. låg
partnertillgång, kan bidra till negativa psykologiska konsekvenser för individer. Dessutom kan
partnertillgångsskalan anses vara ett reliabelt och validt mått som kunde införlivas i evolutionäroch psykologisk forskning. Partnertillgångsskalan kunde användas som ett förklarande
komplement till skalor som avser mäta t.ex. självrapporterat partnervärde. Framtida studier kunde
validera skalan med andra sampel, eftersom självrapporterat partnervärde, negativ affekt och
partnertillgång kan tänkas samvariera på andra sätt i mycket större städer eller i mycket mindre
byar.

Negativ affekt verkar vara förknippat med svårigheter att hitta en partner, Pro gradu-avhandling i
psykologi
Fakulteten för humanoria, psykologi och teologi, Åbo Akademi
Resultaten från en pro-gradu avhandling i psykologi vid Åbo Akademi tyder på att negativ affekt
är förknippat med svårigheter att hitta sexuella/romantiska partners. Dessa fynd tyder på att
människor som upplever att de träffar få personer eller upplever få parningsmöjligheter med
personer som de med avseende på deras kön, ålder och personlighet skulle kunna vara
sexuellt/romantiskt intresserade av upplever mer negativ affekt. Studien utfördes i form av en
nätenkät. 528 personer i åldern 18 till 77 deltog i studien. Syftet med studien var att utveckla ett
frågeformulär för partnertillgänglighet och resultaten tyder på att partnertillgänglighetsskalan kan
anses vara ett reliabelt och validt mått. Partnertillgänglighetsskalan kunde användas som ett
förklarande komplement till skalor som avser mäta t.ex. självrapporterat partnervärde. Framtida
studier kunde dock validera skalan med andra sampel, eftersom partnertillgänglighet och negativ
affekt kan tänkas samvariera på andra sätt i mycket större städer eller mycket mindre byar.
